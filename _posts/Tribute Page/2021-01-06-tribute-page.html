The NFL, the Erdős Number, and a Whacky, Homeless Mathematician

This story starts with John Urschel - the 6' 3" tall, 313 pound, fifth round draft pick in the 2014 NFL draft. He was a guard from Penn State, and he was good too. But what had me on Urschel's Wikipedia page wasn't his spot in the draft - it was his Wonderlic score.

The NFL administers a sort of IQ test called the Wonderlic to every player at the NFL combine, which, if you know nothing about football, is sort of like an interview for players seeking to be drafted - the players display their athletic and cognitive abilities through a battery of physical tests and one coginitive test, the Wonderlic. For better or worse, the NFL publishes every player's Wonderlic score. Many players are famous for their very low scores, but most players are, according to the Wonderlic at least, of average intelligence. But John Urschel's score was remarkably high: he scored 43 out of 50 possible points. This is a 99th percentile score, which indicates an IQ of about 143.

The story gets even better, though. Urschel announced his retirement from the NFL in 2017 after three seasons with the Baltimore Ravens. He had started a Ph.D. in mathematics at MIT during the 2016 season and quit football to pursue the degree as a full-time student. Forbes wrote of Urschel, "Urschel has published six peer-reviewed mathematics papers to date and has three more ready for review. That's a respectable publication history for someone who only started pursuing their PhD at MIT this year. He's won academic awards for his math prowess. All this while playing guard for the Baltimore Ravens."

Everything I read about Urschel was interesting, but what grabbed my attention the most was the last sentence of his Wikipedia page: "As of 2017, Urschel has an Erdős number of 4."

The Erdős Number

Wikipedia was, of course, inviting me to explore The Erdős Number. Those three words, underlined and colored blue, were too much for me to resist. I had to go one level deeper into the rabbit hole. I had to know more about The Erdős Number. So, I clicked.

The first sentence of the Wikipedia page reads, "The Erdős number describes the 'collaborative distance' between mathematician Paul Erdős and another person, as measured by authorship of mathematical papers." The way I understand it, if Jane has written a paper with Erdős directly, Jane has an Erdős number of 1. If John has written a paper with Jane, but hasn't written a paper with Erdős, then John will have an Erdős number of 2. And so on and on.

The idea of "degrees of separation" has been explored before. "The Six Degrees of Kevin Bacon" relates the idea that every person on planet earth has, at most, six degrees of separation from Kevin Bacon, where 0 degrees knew Kevin Bacon personally, 1 degree knew someone who knew Kevin Bacon, and so on. And this is an interesting sort of game to play, but, still, it's just a game and has little importance beyond being an interesting factoid and the subject of a documentary. The Erdős Number seemed like more or less the same thing as the Six Degrees of Kevin Bacon - except it wasn't.

The Erdős Number was clearly much more important than my initial quick take suggested it was. Look at any modern mathematician's Wikipedia page and you'll find an Erdős Number on every single one of them. The Erdős Number is more than just a fun game - it's almost a yardstick for measuring the importance of a mathematician. But why choose Erdős? Surely there were other important mathematicians to whom modern mathematicians could be linked. Right? Even more surprising about this yardstick was that Erdős himself never actually won the Fields Medal, nor is his work considered very important compared to some other mathematicians of modern history. And, of course, there was the blue underlined Wikipedia link leading me to Erdős' page to explore the question.

Who Was Paul Erdős?

The child of two Hungarian mathematics teachers, Paul Erdős was a child prodigy in the dark arts - he taught himself to read using mathematics textbooks his parents kept at home, proved Chebyshev's theorem at the age of 20, and was awarded a doctorate at the age of 21 from the University of Budapest. Erdős' precocious youth had been marked by the first world war, and his adulthood was to be marked by the second. A Jew in Hitler's Europe, Erdős was uprooted and never again had a real home. He lived an itinerant lifestyle - "out of a suitcase" as some describe it - traveling to the homes of mathematicians across the world, collaborating with them on mathematics papers, and moving on to the home of the next mathematician.

Since his main act of survival was writing mathematics, it's no surprise that Erdős was extremely prolific. He is, in fact, the most prolific mathematician who ever lived, when measured by number of number of papers written. He is also, again unsurprisingly, the most collaborative. Erdős is known to have written over 1,500 articles with over 500 collaborators. This is most likely why Erdős is used as one standard to measure mathematicians. It may seem silly to measure a mathematician by collabortive distance to one particularly collaborative, if not important, mathematician, but the Erdős Number actually seems to be a good predictor of a mathematician's importance: leading mathematicians tend to have lower Erdős Numbers. According to one study, the median Erdős number for a Fields Medal winner is 3.
